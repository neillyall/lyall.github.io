Course Outline and Some Supplementary Class Notes

For the most part we shall follow closely the appropriate sections of the reference(s) listed above.
  • Introduction: A premeasure on certain subsets of (0,1], sets of measure zero, and the (Baby) Borel Normal Number Theorem  (Section 1.1 in [1], see also Section 1.1 in [2])

    • Rademacher functions and the Weak Law of Large Numbers (including a proof of "Baby Chebyshev")
    • Sets of Measure Zero (null sets) and the Strong Law of Large Numbers
    • A Technical Lemma about Length and consequences:
      • m_0 is well-defined and finitely additive on B_0
      • Non-empty h-intervals are not null
      • m_0 is countably additive on B_0 (it is indeed a premeasure)

Problem Set 2: Sets of Measure Zero
 
  • Measure Theory (We will essentially follow the presentation in Chapter 1 of [4] - see Section 1.2 and Section 1.3 in [1] for a similar presentation for Probability Measures only)
    • Properties of a premeasure defined on an algebra (field) of sets: monontonicity, subadditivity, and continuity from above and below
    • Measure and sigma-algebras (sigma-fields)
      • The Borel sigma-algebra
    • Caratheodory's Theorem: Outer measure, the notion of a set being measurable, and proof that the collection of measurable sets forms a a sigma-algebra
    • Application 1: Caratheodory's Extension Theorem
      • Any premeasure on an algebra A can be extended to a measure on the sigma-algebra M generated by A
        • This extension is unique under certain (natural) conditions
      • Example: Borel measures on the real line
    • Application 2: Metric outer measures and Borel sets
      • Metric outer measures restricted to Borel sets are measures
        • Regularity of Borel measures
      • Examples:
        • Lebesgue measure on R^d (including uniqueness)
          • Translation, dilation, and rotational invariance
        • Borel measures on the real line (alternative approach)
